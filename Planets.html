What is a Planet?

Definition

A planet is a celestial body that is:

  • In Orbit Around a Star
  • Massive Enough To Be Rounded By its Own Gravity
  • and has cleared the neighborhood around its orbit of other debris.

This definition adopted by the International Astronomical Union (IAU) in 2006, has been the subject of some debate, particularly regarding the third criterion. It led to the Reclassification of Pluto as a "dwarf planet."

Dwarf Planets

Dwarf planets meet the first two criteria of the planet definition (Orbiting a star and being rounded With Star By Gravity) But Have *not* Cleared Their Neighborhood. Pluto is the most well-known example. Other dwarf planets include Ceres, Eris, Haumea and Makemake.

Exoplanets

Planets That Orbit Stars Other Than Our Sun are Called Exoplanets. Thousands of exoplanets have been discovered, and this is a very active area of astronomical research.

Formation of Planets

Planets are thought to form from the protoplanetary disks of gas and dust that surround young stars. Over time, these materials clump together and grow through accretion, eventually forming planetesimals, then protoplanets, and finally planets.

Types of Planets

Terrestrial Planets (Rocky Planets)

These are planets like Earth and Mars, with solid, rocky surfaces.

Gas Giants

These are large planets like Jupiter and Saturn, composed mostly of hydrogen and helium.

Ice Giants

These are planets like Uranus and Neptune, which are also large but contain a higher proportion of heavier elements ("ices") than gas giants.

Super-Earths

These are exoplanets that are more massive than Earth but less massive than Neptune.

Mini-Neptunes

These are exoplanets that are smaller than Neptune but larger than Earth.
