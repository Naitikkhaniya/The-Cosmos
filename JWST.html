James Webb Space Telescope

What is JWST?

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is the most powerful space telescope ever built. It is designed to observe the universe in infrared light, allowing it to see through dust and gas clouds and witness the formation of the first stars and galaxies. It is a successor to the Hubble Space Telescope and offers significantly improved capabilities.

JWST is a joint project of NASA, the European Space Agency (ESA), and the Canadian Space Agency (CSA). It was launched on December 25, 2021, and is now orbiting the Sun at the second Lagrange point (L2), about 1.5 million kilometers (930,000 miles) from Earth.

First Images

JWST released its first full-color images and spectroscopic data on July 12, 2022. These images showcased the telescope's incredible capabilities and provided a glimpse into the early universe, distant galaxies, and exoplanetary systems. The images were met with excitement and awe from scientists and the public alike.

These initial images are just the beginning of JWST's exploration of the cosmos. Over its mission, it is expected to revolutionize our understanding of the universe.

Scientific Goals

JWST has four primary scientific goals:

  • To observe the first stars and galaxies that formed after the Big Bang.
  • To study the formation and evolution of galaxies.
  • To observe the formation of stars and planetary systems.
  • To analyze the atmospheres of exoplanets for signs of life.

Orbit and Instrumentation

JWST orbits the Sun at the second Lagrange point (L2), which is a stable location about 1.5 million kilometers (930,000 miles) from Earth. This allows the telescope to have an unobstructed view of the universe.

JWST is equipped with four scientific instruments: a Near-Infrared Camera (NIRCam), a Mid-Infrared Instrument (MIRI), a Near-Infrared Spectrograph (NIRSpec), and a Fine Guidance Sensor/Near-Infrared Imager and Slitless Spectrograph (FGS/NIRISS). These instruments work together to capture high-resolution images and spectra of celestial objects.
